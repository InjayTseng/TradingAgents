## 2. 🎯 The Forward P/E of 17.6x Is Built on Assumptions That May Not Materialize

This is the centerpiece of the bull argument, so let me give it the scrutiny it deserves. My colleague argues that the gap between TTM EPS of $5.12 and Forward EPS of $17.67 represents "real dynamics" — amortization roll-off and AI compounding. **First, that $17.67 forward EPS requires nearly a 3.5x increase in earnings from current TTM levels.** That is an extraordinary assumption. Yes, VMware amortization will roll off — but amortization charges don't disappear overnight. They wind down over years based on the useful life schedules of acquired intangibles, and VMware's $128 billion intangible base will continue generating meaningful amortization charges for several more years. The "dramatic" earnings normalization the bulls promise is more gradual than the forward estimates imply.

**Second, consensus analyst forward estimates have a well-documented history of being systematically over-optimistic,** particularly for high-growth tech companies during periods of AI hype. The same analysts who projected Nvidia's earnings 18 months out were wildly off. The same dynamic applies here. Forward EPS of $17.67 embeds assumptions about hyperscaler AI capex continuing to accelerate, VMware conversion executing flawlessly, and macro conditions remaining benign. Remove any one of those assumptions, and that multiple expands dramatically.

**Third — and this is critical — if the forward EPS estimates are even 20% too optimistic**, you're buying AVGO at roughly 22x forward earnings rather than 17.6x. That's a completely different valuation story for a semiconductor company in a cyclical industry with $66 billion in debt.

## 3. ⚠️ Hyperscaler Concentration Risk Is Severely Underappreciated by Bulls

My colleague acknowledges concentration risk almost in passing, describing it as a "small number of hyperscaler XPU customers." Let me translate that into what it actually means for the investment thesis.

AVGO's AI semiconductor growth story is essentially dependent on sustained, accelerating capital expenditure from **Google, Meta, and Apple.** These are three companies. If Google decides to redesign its TPU architecture in-house, if Meta pivots its MTIA strategy, or if Apple's AI chip roadmap shifts — the revenue impact on AVGO would be severe and immediate. This isn't theoretical risk; it's the nature of custom silicon relationships. They are deep, but they are not permanent.

Here's the bear scenario that keeps me up at night: **hyperscaler AI capex digestion.** These companies have been spending at unprecedented rates. At some point — and it may be sooner than bulls expect — there's a natural absorption period where the clusters they've built need to generate returns before the next wave of spend is authorized. Any indication of capex digestion from even *one* of these hyperscalers would crater near-term AVGO estimates and the stock would reprice violently.

The $60-90 billion TAM projection my colleague cited for the XPU business? That's an aspiration, not a commitment. And aspirations don't show up in quarterly EPS prints. When the market realizes the gap between the TAM story and near-term revenue delivery, multiple compression is the inevitable result.

## 4. 💣 $66 Billion in Debt Is Not as Manageable as Bulls Suggest

My colleague frames the 2.5x debt-to-FCF ratio as "entirely manageable" and suggests the debt becomes "increasingly irrelevant" within 2-3 years. I want to challenge both of those conclusions.

**At $66 billion in total debt, Broadcom is one of the most leveraged large-cap technology companies in the world.** Yes, $26.9B in annual FCF provides coverage — but that FCF isn't all going toward debt repayment. In Q1 FY2026 alone, they returned $10.94 billion to shareholders while only modestly reducing net debt. The de-levering pace is much slower than the bull presentation implies.

Let's do the math: net debt is $51.9B. If AVGO dedicates 50% of annual FCF (~$13.5B) to debt repayment, it takes nearly **4 years** just to pay off the net debt — and that assumes FCF doesn't decline, capex doesn't rise, and no new acquisitions occur. Hock Tan's track record is that of a *serial acquirer*. Suggesting he'll stay on the sidelines for 4 years while peacefully paying down debt is inconsistent with his entire history of building this company.

Meanwhile, **the interest rate environment creates real drag.** $66B in debt at even 4-5% average interest costs is $2.6-3.3B in annual interest expense — a significant headwind to net income and one that the bear scenario deserves to price more conservatively.

And the intangible-heavy balance sheet deserves more respect as a risk factor. **Negative tangible book value of -$48.2 billion means that in any stress scenario — a major customer loss, a competitive disruption, a recession — there is essentially no hard asset value protecting debt holders, let alone equity holders.** This isn't immaterial. It's a fundamental structural vulnerability that the bull argument dismisses too quickly.

## 5. 🏭 VMware Integration: The Execution Risk Is Real and Ongoing

My colleague paints VMware as a "compounding moat" story. I see it differently — at least in the near-to-medium term. Let me explain why the integration risk is being underappreciated.

Broadcom's strategy of transitioning VMware customers from perpetual licenses to subscription contracts sounds elegant in a pitch deck. In practice, it has generated significant customer friction, enterprise backlash, and competitive opportunity for alternatives. **Competitors like Nutanix have been explicitly marketing against Broadcom's VMware pricing strategy**, and by all accounts, some enterprise customers are actively exploring migration paths.

The $128B in goodwill and intangibles sitting on the balance sheet essentially represents the bet that VMware's customer base is captive and monetizable. If even 10-15% of VMware's 350,000+ customers meaningfully reduce their AVGO footprint due to pricing dissatisfaction, the impairment risk on that goodwill becomes a real conversation. A goodwill impairment charge against a $128B intangible base could be *catastrophic* to reported earnings and would dramatically re-rate the stock regardless of underlying FCF.

The Adobe/Salesforce comparison my colleague made is actually instructive — but not in the way he intends. Adobe's Creative Cloud transition took years and faced significant pushback. Salesforce's growth has decelerated meaningfully as the market matured. These aren't stories of frictionless, linear compounding — they're stories of difficult transitions with real execution risk.

## 6. 📊 The "Buyback Signal" Argument Has a Dark Side

My colleague makes much of management's $7.85 billion in Q1 FY2026 buybacks as a "signal" that the stock is undervalued. I want to offer a more skeptical read of this.

**First, management buybacks have an extremely mixed track record as valuation signals.** Companies buy back stock at all prices — including at the very top of cycles. If buybacks were reliable timing signals, companies wouldn't have so systematically bought back their own stock at 2021 peak valuations only to watch it crater 30-50% afterward. AVGO itself was buying back stock as the stock fell from $412 to $310. That's not a prescient value signal — that's a pre-committed capital allocation program executing regardless of price action.

**Second, those buybacks are being partially debt-funded.** In Q1 FY2026, AVGO *issued* $4.47B in new debt while repaying $3.65B — a net increase. They're returning $10.94B to shareholders while running a net borrowing position. In a bull market, this is called "financial engineering." In a bear market, it's called "leveraging up at the top."

**Third, at $310/share with a TTM P/E of 60.6x**, buying back stock is extraordinarily expensive on any trailing earnings basis. If the forward EPS estimate proves over-optimistic, these buybacks will have destroyed significant shareholder value rather than creating it.

## 4. ⚠️ Hyperscaler Relationships — The Bull Made My Point About Switching Costs, Then Forgot What It Implies

For next-generation custom silicon, every hyperscaler evaluates their options with each new design cycle.

And here's a competitive dynamic the bull completely ignored: **Intel, Marvell, and increasingly in-house design capabilities at the hyperscalers themselves are all competing for that next design win.** Amazon has Trainium and Inferentia, developed in-house. Microsoft has been developing custom silicon partnerships. The custom silicon market isn't Broadcom's permanent domain — it's a competitive arena where they must win each design cycle. The current revenue acceleration reflects *current* design wins. Whether it reflects *future* design wins is an open question the bull dismisses too casually.

The revenue growth looks great right now — I've never disputed that. My concern is whether that growth rate is *sustainable* or whether we're seeing peak custom silicon revenue from the current design cycle before the natural consolidation and renegotiation of the next cycle.

## 6. 🏭 On VMware — The Numbers Look Good Now, But The Lagging Indicator Problem Is Real
